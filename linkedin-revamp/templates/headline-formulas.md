# Headline formulas

Six reusable headline structures that work for any role. Pick by `voice_mode` and `target_person`.

Every headline must:
- Fit in 220 characters (LinkedIn limit)
- Include the role or function the user wants to be associated with
- Include 2–3 keywords that the `target_person` would search for
- Lead with the strongest claim, not the company

---

## Formula 1 — Builder forward

> `[Verb] [Outcome] for [Audience] · [Specialty 1] · [Specialty 2] · [City]`

When to use: voice_mode is "Confident" or "Bold". target_person is a peer, client, or hiring manager. north_star is about authority or building.

Examples:
- "Building data pipelines that 50+ analysts rely on · Python · dbt · Snowflake · Toronto"
- "Shipping React component libraries that 50+ design teams use · Frontend systems · TypeScript · Berlin"
- "Closing $1M+ in B2B SaaS deals for Series A founders · Outbound · enterprise · NYC"

---

## Formula 2 — Role at company + specialty

> `[Role] @ [Company] · [Specialty cluster] · [Anchor credential or audience]`

When to use: target_person is a recruiter. User is currently employed and the company name has weight.

Examples:
- "Senior PM @ Stripe · Payments infrastructure · API design · ex-Plaid"
- "Engineering Lead @ Notion · Database internals · Distributed systems · ex-Airbnb"

---

## Formula 3 — I do X for Y

> `I [Verb] [Outcome] for [Specific audience] · [Proof point] · [City]`

When to use: voice_mode is "Bold". Independent consultant, founder, or freelancer. Strong target_person clarity.

Examples:
- "I help YC founders raise their seed round in 90 days · 6 closed in 2025 · SF"
- "I design B2B SaaS dashboards that users actually open · 12 shipped this year · Lisbon"

---

## Formula 4 — Curiosity hook

> `[Provocative claim or unconventional positioning] · [What I do] · [City]`

When to use: voice_mode is "Bold" or "Funny". User has a north_star that lets them stand out.

Examples:
- "Turning support tickets into product roadmaps · Product @ a SaaS scaleup · Tokyo"
- "Designing for users who hate enterprise software · Senior product designer · Tokyo"

---

## Formula 5 — Max search keywords

> `[Role] · [Specialty 1] · [Specialty 2] · [Specialty 3] · [Specialty 4] · [City]`

When to use: target_person is a recruiter scanning many profiles. Keyword density beats poetry.

Examples:
- "Data Engineer · ETL · Python · Spark · Airflow · dbt · Snowflake · Toronto"
- "Senior iOS Engineer · Swift · SwiftUI · Combine · Core Data · CI/CD · Stockholm"

---

## Formula 6 — Career switcher signal

> `[New direction I'm headed] · ex-[Old direction] · [Anchor credential] · [Why I'm credible in new direction]`

When to use: User is mid-transition. north_star and current Experience don't match.

Examples:
- "Learning to build data products · ex-finance analyst · Spent the last 4 months shipping 6 dashboards · Toronto"
- "Becoming a product designer · ex-finance · Just finished Designership · Toronto"

## Positioning structure (use before picking a formula)

A headline formula gives you the shape. This gives you the substance. Run the user's material through one of these two structures first, then pour the result into whichever formula fits the voice. Both are adapted from product positioning, pointed at a person instead of a product.

### Category-of-one line

> `I'm the [category] for [specific audience] who want [specific outcome].`

This forces a narrow claim. "Engineer" is a category of millions. "The reliability engineer for early-stage fintechs who want to stop getting paged at 3am" is a category of one. Narrow wins on LinkedIn because the right reader feels personally addressed.

Examples (synthetic):
- "I'm the lifecycle marketer for B2B SaaS teams who want activation, not just signups."
- "I'm the data engineer for analytics teams who are tired of pipelines that break silently."

### Value-prop line

> `For [target] who [problem], I [benefit]. Unlike [others], I [differentiator].`

Longer, sharper. Best as the seed for an About hook or a Featured-tile description, but it also compresses into a headline once you strip it down. The `Unlike` clause is the part most people skip, and it's the part that does the work.

Examples (synthetic):
- "For founders who can't tell why users churn, I turn raw product data into a churn story they can act on. Unlike most analysts, I ship the fix, not just the dashboard."
- "For design teams drowning in inconsistent components, I build the system that ends the debate. Unlike agencies, I stay to maintain it."

How to use: write the value-prop line in full during planning. It clarifies the positioning. Then compress it into the headline (drop the `Unlike` clause if you're tight on the 220-char limit) and keep the full version as raw material for the About hook.

## Before → After rewrites

Before is the first-pass headline a generic AI produces. After is what survives the rules below. All synthetic. Study the move in each pair.

### Before → After

Before:
> "Experienced Software Engineer | Passionate about building innovative solutions | Team player"

After:
> "Building payment systems that don't drop transactions · Go · Postgres · distributed systems · Austin"

The Before is three empty claims and a pipe. The After leads with a concrete outcome, stacks the searchable keywords, and adds a city.

Before:
> "Results-driven marketing professional with a proven track record of driving growth"

After:
> "I grow B2B newsletters from zero to 10k without paid spend · lifecycle + content · 3 done this year"

"Results-driven", "proven track record", "driving growth" all vanish. The After makes one specific, checkable claim and shows it's been done more than once.

Before:
> "Senior Consultant helping organizations leverage data to unlock their full potential"

After:
> "I turn messy product data into churn fixes for Series-B SaaS · ex-McKinsey-style rigor, in-house speed · NYC"

"Leverage" and "unlock their full potential" are pure fluff. The After names the audience, the outcome, and a differentiator in the same line.

Before:
> "Aspiring Product Manager | Recent Graduate | Eager to learn and make an impact"

After:
> "Breaking into product from support · Shipped 2 internal tools my team uses daily · ex-CX lead"

"Aspiring", "eager to learn", "make an impact" signal junior. The After shows a credible bridge from a real prior role with real proof.

## Rules of thumb across all formulas

1. **No filler verbs.** Skip "passionate about", "interested in", "looking to". Lead with action: "Building", "Shipping", "Designing", "Closing".
2. **No job title without proof.** "Senior Engineer" means nothing alone. Add a specialty or company.
3. **City matters for hiring.** Always include if user wants local opportunities.
4. **"Open to Work" goes elsewhere.** Use the LinkedIn frame, not the headline.
5. **Past credentials use `ex-`.** "ex-Google" lands harder than "Previously at Google".
6. **Mid-dot `·` over pipe `|`.** Cleaner. More widely supported.
7. **No emojis unless voice_mode is "Funny".** They flag as junior or salesy.

---

## Anti-patterns to never produce

- "Passionate about technology and innovation"
- "Open to new opportunities"
- "Helping companies grow through strategic thinking"
- "Award-winning [anything]"
- "Thought leader in [field]"
- "Synergy / leverage / pivot / disruptor / unicorn / rockstar / ninja / wizard / guru"
- Anything that ends with "...and so much more"
- Job title with no specialty or proof point

If any of these appears in a draft, regenerate.
